Most Valuable US Error Coins: What to Look For

Updated March 2025 · By CoinWisdom Editorial · 11 min read

Every coin collector dreams of finding a valuable mint error in pocket change. Doubled dies, off-center strikes, wrong planchet coins, and other mint mistakes can turn an ordinary coin into something worth hundreds or thousands of dollars. The remarkable thing is that these errors are still being discovered today — in old coin jars, estate collections, and occasionally even fresh from the bank.

This guide covers the most valuable error types, how to identify them, and what they're worth in today's market.

Types of US Mint Errors

1. Doubled Die Errors

A doubled die occurs when a working die receives more than one impression from the master hub, with slight misalignment between impressions. The result is a coin that appears to have doubled lettering, dates, or design elements. Unlike machine doubling (worthless), true hub doubling creates distinct, separated impressions.

2. Off-Center Strikes

An off-center strike happens when the planchet (coin blank) is not properly centered between the dies when struck. The result is a coin with part of the design missing and an irregular shape. The more dramatically off-center, the more valuable — as long as the date is visible.

The value drops significantly if the date is missing — collectors need to know the year to attribute the coin properly. A 50% off-center Lincoln cent without the date might bring $50, while the same coin with a clear date could fetch $200–$400.

3. Wrong Planchet Errors

These dramatic errors occur when a coin blank (planchet) intended for one denomination accidentally gets struck by dies for a different denomination. For example, a cent planchet struck by quarter dies, or a dime planchet struck by cent dies. The size mismatch makes these instantly obvious and highly collectible.

4. Broadstruck Coins

When the retaining collar (which gives a coin its round shape and reeded edge) is absent during striking, the metal spreads outward beyond the normal diameter. These "broadstrikes" are larger than normal, thin, and often lack edge reeding. Values range from $20–$50 for cents to $200–$600 for quarters and halves.

5. Clipped Planchet Errors

A clipped planchet results from the blanking press punching out a coin blank that overlapped the edge of the metal strip or a previously punched hole. The result is a coin with a curved "bite" taken out of it. Straight clips (from strip edges) and curved clips (from punch overlaps) are both collectible. Values range from $15 for cent clips to $100–$500 for large denomination clips.

6. Die Cap Errors

In a die cap error, a coin sticks to a die and becomes a "cap" that strikes subsequent planchets, creating dramatically deformed coins. The cap coins and the pieces they strike both become errors. These are among the most visually dramatic errors and can bring $500–$5,000+ depending on denomination and drama of the deformation.

Modern Error Coins Still Found in Change

Error coins are still being made today — quality control has improved but not eliminated minting mistakes. Here are recent errors worth checking your change for:

State Quarters (1999–2008)

The 2004-D Wisconsin quarter has an extra leaf variety on the ear of corn — either a high leaf or low leaf. Both are genuine die errors that escaped quality control and sell for $100–$400 in circulated condition. Billions of state quarters were made, but the error variety is genuinely scarce.

Presidential Dollar Edge Lettering Errors

Presidential dollars (2007–2016) had their edge lettering ("IN GOD WE TRUST," year, and mint mark) applied separately. Some escaped without any edge lettering — the so-called "Godless Dollars." In 2007, these were found in rolls and bags at banks. Today they sell for $50–$200 depending on the president and condition.

Sacagawea/State Quarter Mule

In 2000, an extraordinary error was discovered: Sacagawea dollar obverses struck with state quarter reverses, on quarter-weight planchets. Only a handful are known. One sold for over $100,000 at auction. These are museum-piece rarities.

How to Authenticate Error Coins

Important: Altered coins — damaged coins made to look like errors — are common. A genuine doubled die shows hub doubling in the die itself; an altered coin has mechanical damage or die polishing marks. For any error coin worth more than $50, professional authentication from PCGS or NGC is strongly recommended.

Red flags for altered coins:

  • Off-center coins with suspiciously clean, straight cuts (post-mint damage)
  • Doubled date that looks like a scratch or "thumbprint" smear
  • Wrong planchet coins that seem to have been altered or trimmed
  • Any coin where the claimed error dramatically increases value

Building an Error Coin Collection

Error coin collecting is one of numismatics' most exciting niches because new errors are constantly being discovered. A smart approach for new collectors:

Start with affordable modern errors — broadstrikes, clips, and minor doubled dies can be bought for $20–$100 and are excellent for learning to authenticate. Work up to more valuable pieces as your eye develops. Join error coin collecting communities (CONECA — Combined Organizations of Numismatic Error Collectors of America — is the main club).

When buying online, scrutinize photos carefully. For anything over $100, only buy PCGS or NGC certified examples unless you're buying from a known, reputable specialist dealer.
